How to search the database?

You can explore the database through the home page by clicking on any cancer type. This will open a detailed view of biomarkers associated with that cancer.

How to interpret the results?

Each biomarker includes the gene symbol, full gene name, biological function, and its impact on a specific cancer type. Clicking the gene symbol will take you to a detailed biomarker page with mutation type, clinical implication, drug association, and more.

How was the data collected?

A broad literature search was conducted to gather information on cancer biomarkers and existing databases. To ensure a high standard of data quality, we employed a structured curation process that involves multiple verification steps. Literature searches using Boolean operators were performed in the PubMed database using keywords such as “cancer” and “biomarker”, along with their synonyms, to refine the search and focus on peer-reviewed journal articles with established impact factors. Only full-text publications were considered to ensure the reliability of the data.

For a biomarker to be included in the database, it had to be cited in a minimum number of high-quality studies and, when applicable, recognized by institutions the National Cancer Institute (NCI) and the American Cancer Society, to ensure accuracy and comprehensiveness.

How was the data standardized and annotated?

To ensure consistency and interoperability, data standardization was a critical component of the curation process. We focused on literature describing molecular biomarkers for human cancers, specifically lung, breast, ovarian, colorectal, prostate, and skin cancers. Each biomarker entry was carefully structured to capture its relationship with cancer type and reference details comprehensively.

For standardization, cancer types were classified using the International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes, providing a globally recognized framework for categorizing diseases. Biomarkers were recorded using their official gene symbols and names as defined by the HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee (HGNC), ensuring uniformity across entries. This approach prevents inconsistencies arising from variations in gene naming conventions, such as abbreviations or outdated nomenclature.
